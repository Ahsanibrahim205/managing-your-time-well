Managing your time well at work

  • You can also devise creative ways of restructuring your concept of time at work. Purposely try to find ways to practise the skills of patient behaviour that suits you. Here are some ideas to get you started:
  • If you arrive at your office in the mornings and find yourself rushing, compulsively going through incoming e-mails, checking for messages, you can make a conscious effort to be less driven.
  • For example delay opening up your e-mails for a time, perhaps even until just after lunch unless to do so would cause a real disturbance to your work performance not just a perceived one. So, if longer delays are not possible, try delays of about 15 minutes.
  • Make this a regular habit and it will enable you to break free from time-obsessive patterns. This behaviour will enable you to get more of a sense of choosing what to do rather than compulsively having to do it. Then you can relax more during the work and still be efficient.
  • Make sure that you regularly take your lunch break. Time how long you require to eat a midday meal and then deliberately order the same meal the following week and spend twice the time to eat it
  • If you catch yourself looking at the watch constantly, commit yourself to writing “I don't have to reassure myself about the time every 2 minutes” and write that 30 times.
  • Learn to prioritise, get into the routine of asking yourself, whether a specific piece of work really has to be dealt with immediately. You can still choose to do it but you don’t have to do it now, so sometimes choose not to do it, and schedule it for later.
  • Recognise and tell yourself that much of your eagerness and thoughts such as “I must act immediately. I have to do it now, later will never do” can be challenged. These responses have been learnt by you. They have grown out of an unconscious conditioning to attend to everything at once as if it were of the utmost importance.
